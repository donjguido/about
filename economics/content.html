Interest-Based Economics

By Julian Guidote · 8 July 2026

The theory is my own. Claude (Anthropic) assisted with locating, checking, and formatting the sources that connect it to the existing literature, which remain under my review (see the note below §Bibliography).

Interest-based economics is a proposed framework that takes the object of economics to be neither goods nor money nor utility in the technical sense, but the interests of agents, and takes an economy to be whatever arrangement a population of agents uses to satisfy them. Its normative core, named interestism, is the obligation to satisfy our own and other agents' interests. The descriptive theory holds that preferences are the observable trace of underlying interests; that interests are hierarchical, can be intrinsic or extrinsic, known or unknown, and are fundamentally linked to needs; that buying is only one route to satisfying an interest, since interests can be provided for directly; and that money is not constitutive of exchange but a coordination technology whose value derives from the scarcity of information in the ecosystem it serves. The entry situates the framework in the history of economic thought (need and subjective value, the marginal revolution, the modern needs and capability traditions), shows how it is latent beneath revealed-preference theory, Hayek's account of prices, and the theory of public goods, and argues that its most appropriate application may lie ahead, in agent-based economies where artificial agents lack stable interests and where extreme wealth makes the direct provision of basic human interests more attractive than their mediation through money.

1. The Thesis: An Economy as a System for Satisfying Interests

Interest-based economics begins from a single reorientation. It takes the object of economics to be neither goods, nor money, nor even utility in the technical sense, but interests, and it takes an economy to be whatever arrangement a population of agents uses to satisfy them. The framework's normative core, which it names interestism, is the moral obligation to satisfy our own and other agents' interests. The descriptive theory that surrounds this obligation holds that economies are driven by interests, that interests are borne by agents, and that the satisfaction of interests is what choices are ultimately for.

The primitives are defined austerely. An agent is any entity that possesses a preference, that is, any entity that, presented with a choice of a given kind, chooses consistently one way rather than another; the agent is the chooser. A preference is a disposition to choose consistently across choices of a similar kind, and preferences are treated not as brute data but as indicators: they are the observable trace of an underlying interest. An interest is that for the sake of which the choice is made. On this account interests can be intrinsic or extrinsic, can be known or unknown even to the agent that holds them, can be manipulated, and stand in a hierarchy; at bottom they are linked to needs. Interests are non-exclusive, so that two agents can hold the same interest without rivalry; and one party frequently does not know the interests of its counterparty, though sometimes a party knows another's interest better than that party knows it itself.

Two commitments distinguish the view from ordinary consumer theory at the outset. First, buying is only one route to the satisfaction of an interest, and not a privileged one: an interest can be provided for directly, as an interest in literacy is provided for by education rather than by any particular purchase. Second, money is not treated as constitutive of the economy but as a technology. Money works because it lets agents satisfy the largest number of interests while carrying the least coordination and logistics burden in an ecosystem where information is scarce; it is an intermediary, and an intermediary is precisely the sort of thing that can be manipulated. The corollary, developed in sections 3 and 4, is that money's usefulness is contingent on the scarcity of information rather than essential to exchange as such.

The theory's account of welfare follows the same shape. Happiness is analysed as a function of three quantities: the interest itself, its satisfaction, and coverage, the proportion of an agent's interests that are met; and it is a function also of an agent's capacity to be satisfied at all. Satisfaction, on this reading, just is the coverage of one's interests. The account leaves room for an agent to hold an interest in being dissatisfied, an interest that can in turn be tied to an interest in growth. The framework is therefore not straightforwardly hedonic: it identifies the good not with a felt quantity of pleasure but with the faithful coverage of what an agent is actually for.

2. Interests in the History of Economic Thought

The claim that economic life is organized around the satisfaction of interests is not new; it recurs, under other names, across the history of the discipline. The framework's contribution is not to have discovered the idea but to make it the primitive and to draw out its consequences. This section traces the lineage; section 3 shows where the same idea sits latent inside theories built for other purposes.

2.1 Need and the Origin of Money

The oldest statement of the view is Aristotle's. In the Nicomachean Ethics V.5 he argues that what makes otherwise incommensurable goods exchangeable is chreia, need or demand: need is the single measure that binds a community of exchangers, and money is a conventional stand-in for it. Aristotle draws the etymological point that money (nomisma) is so called because it exists not by nature but by convention or law (nomos), and can therefore be altered or rendered useless by agreement (Aristotle, Nicomachean Ethics V.5, 1133a25–31; see also Politics I.9 on the emergence of exchange and coined money). Scott Meikle's study of these passages reconstructs the argument that demand, not labor or intrinsic worth, is what does the commensurating (Meikle 1995). The structure is already the framework's own: interests (needs) are the fundamental term, and money is a conventional intermediary standing in for them, valuable by agreement rather than by nature.

2.2 The Subjective Turn and the Scope of Economics

The decisive modern move is the marginal revolution of 1871–1874, in which Jevons, Menger, and Walras, working almost simultaneously and independently, relocated the source of value from the cost or labor embodied in a good to the subjective want it satisfies at the margin (Jevons 1871; Menger 1871; Walras 1874). In the German subjective-value tradition that Menger synthesized, human wants and the desire to satisfy them are treated as the driving force of all economic processes, indeed as their first and final cause, and value is located in the mental states of agents rather than in objects (Harper and Endres 2023). This is the framework's claim that preferences reveal interests and that the satisfaction of interests drives choice, stated a century and a half earlier in the vocabulary of utility and want.

If the marginalists supplied the substance, Robbins supplied the frame. His Essay defines economics as “the science which studies human behaviour as a relationship between ends and scarce means which have alternative uses,” and insists that economics is neutral between ends: it takes agents' ends as given data and asks only how scarcity conditions their pursuit (Robbins 1932, ch. I §3, ch. II §2). Interest-based economics slots directly into the space Robbins deliberately brackets. Where he treats ends as exogenous and declines to theorize them, the framework supplies the missing theory of those ends, holding that they are interests, that they are structured and linked to needs, and that their satisfaction is the point of the whole apparatus.

2.3 Needs and Their Satisfiers

A second tradition supplies the framework's claim that interests are hierarchical and linked to needs, and its claim that buying is only one way to satisfy them. Maslow's account of motivation gives the canonical statement that human needs are arranged in a hierarchy of relative prepotency, the emergence of one need typically resting on the prior satisfaction of a more prepotent one (Maslow 1943). The framework borrows the general idea of a needs hierarchy rather than any fixed ordering: the claim that motivation follows an invariant five-tier sequence lacks consistent empirical support, and Maslow himself hedged it (Wahba and Bridwell 1976).

The needs tradition also supplies the framework's sharpest structural distinction. Max-Neef argues that fundamental human needs are finite, few, and the same in all cultures and historical periods; what changes is only the satisfiers, the means by which needs are met. Crucially, there is no one-to-one correspondence between needs and satisfiers: one satisfier can serve several needs, one need may require several satisfiers, and many satisfiers are non-market forms such as institutions, practices, and relationships (Max-Neef 1991). Doyal and Gough press the objectivity of needs, arguing that all persons have universal and objective needs for physical health and autonomy that are not reducible to subjective wants (Doyal and Gough 1991). The needs-versus-satisfiers distinction is exactly the framework's interests-versus-means distinction, and it is what licenses the claim that an interest can be provided for directly, without passing through a market at all.

The capability approach carries the point into welfare measurement. Sen and Nussbaum argue that quality of life should be assessed not by income or by preference-satisfaction but by what people are actually able to do and to be, and Nussbaum casts a list of central capabilities as constitutional entitlements a just government secures directly (Sen 1999; Nussbaum 2000). Nussbaum's critique of adaptive preferences, that the oppressed learn to want less and so register contentment on a preference metric, is itself an argument that revealed preference can mismeasure real interest, a wedge the framework will need in section 3 and again in section 5.

2.4 A Worked Example: The Feudal Economy

Read through the framework, a historical economy is a structure for the satisfaction, and frequently the frustration, of the interests of differently situated agents. Feudalism resolves into roughly three bodies of agents whose interests differ in kind: the interests of kings centered on the preservation of power, those of serfs on survival, and those of the class between on upward mobility. What distinguishes that economy from a modern one is not that serfs lacked an interest in rising but that the interest was, for most, unrealizable: mobility required skills of trade and politics that circumstance and, in some cases, the guilds' engineered monopoly on knowledge kept out of reach. The one ordinary avenue by which a serf might suddenly acquire new skills and a claim on reward was war, a high-variance wager rational only because the alternative, a life of very low value, made the expected return on risking it favorable. The example illustrates the framework's contention that the binding constraint in an economy is often not the supply of goods but the coverage of interests that structure permits, and that expanding whose interests can be realized, rather than raising throughput, is the relevant kind of progress.

3. The Latent Theory

The framework's stronger historical claim is not merely that interest-based ideas have recurred but that they lie latent inside theories built for other purposes, doing work their authors did not always name. Four bodies of formal theory are read this way.

3.1 Choices Reveal Interests

Samuelson's revealed-preference program set out to rebuild consumer theory on observable choice alone. In its most ambitious form it aimed to eliminate the terms “preference” and “utility” from the theory altogether and to re-ground it in demand behavior, with the Weak Axiom of Revealed Preference as a pure consistency condition: if a bundle is chosen when another was affordable, consistency constrains what may be chosen when prices change (Samuelson 1938; Hands 2014). Samuelson later reconciled the program with ordinal utility (Samuelson 1948), and the eliminationist reading is contested, but the operative idea is the framework's own: an interest is read off consistent choice, and an agent simply is an entity whose choices are consistent enough to reveal one. The dependence runs the other way too. Because the theory recovers interest only from consistent choice, it is silent about agents whose choices are not consistent, a limitation that becomes decisive in section 4.

3.2 Money as Coordination Under Scarce Information

The framework's most distinctive claim, that money is valuable because it economizes coordination in an information-sparse ecosystem, has its deepest precedent in Hayek. Hayek frames the economic problem of society as the use of knowledge that is dispersed among many individuals and never given to any single mind in its totality, and casts the price system as a mechanism for communicating that knowledge, “a kind of machinery for registering change,” whose most significant feature is the economy of knowledge with which it works: how little any participant needs to know in order to act correctly (Hayek 1945, §§I, V, VII). Prices, on this view, are valuable precisely because information is scarce and dispersed; they compress what no agent could gather.

The framework takes one further step, and it is important to mark it as the framework's own rather than Hayek's. If the price signal's value derives from the scarcity and dispersion of information, then in an ecosystem where information is instead abundant and cheaply shared, the intermediary's informational advantage shrinks, and money, as a compressed proxy for interests, loses functional value relative to arrangements that coordinate on interests directly. Hayek himself held the dispersion of knowledge to be a permanent condition of social life and would resist the premise that an information-rich ecosystem is attainable; the inference from his mechanism to money's contingency is an extension the framework draws, not a thesis he endorsed. Section 4 argues that agent-based economies are the first setting in which the premise is even arguably approached.

3.3 Interests Misrepresented and Manipulated

The framework holds that interests can be manipulated and that money, as an intermediary, is vulnerable to manipulation. Two formal results give the claim precise content. Samuelson's theory of public goods shows that for collective-consumption goods, which each agent can enjoy without subtracting from another's enjoyment (later termed non-rival), no decentralized price mechanism can determine the optimal level of provision, because it is in the selfish interest of each agent to give false signals, understating a benefit it cannot be excluded from (Samuelson 1954). The free-rider problem is thus a problem of interests deliberately misrepresented. Mechanism design generalizes it: the Gibbard–Satterthwaite theorem establishes that when there are at least three alternatives, no non-dictatorial rule can make truthful revelation of preference a dominant strategy, so every non-trivial mechanism for eliciting interests is manipulable (Gibbard 1973; Satterthwaite 1975).

Behavioral research adds that revealed interests may not be stable underlying quantities at all. In the constructed-preference program, preferences are frequently built during the act of elicitation rather than read off a pre-existing ordering, and normatively equivalent methods (choosing versus pricing) yield systematically different responses, violating the procedure invariance that rational-choice theory assumes (Lichtenstein and Slovic 2006; Tversky, Slovic, and Kahneman 1990). Framing and response mode drive the construction, which is the lever that framing and choice-architecture interventions exploit. Taken together these results support two of the framework's planks at once: that interests can be manipulated, and that the mapping from revealed preference to real interest is loose enough that a theory of interests cannot simply identify the two.

3.4 The Hierarchy of Interests

The framework's claim that interests are hierarchical has a canonical philosophical statement in Frankfurt. Personhood, on his account, rests on the capacity for reflective self-evaluation, expressed as second-order desires, desires about which first-order desires one wants to have or to act on (Frankfurt 1971). The framework borrows the hierarchical structure, an agent's interests ordered by interests about interests, while declining a stronger attribution: Frankfurt's hierarchy is reflexive, a matter of first- and second-order desire, and does not by itself supply the separate intrinsic-versus-extrinsic taxonomy the framework also employs, which must be grounded elsewhere.

4. Interest-Based Economics for Agent-Based Economies

The framework's forward claim is that its most appropriate application lies ahead, in economies populated wholly or partly by artificial agents. This section is more conjectural than the last two, and the caveat should be stated plainly: the specialized literature on the economics of large-language-model agents is thin and recent, and several of the load-bearing empirical claims below are not yet settled. What the framework offers here is an argument for why interests, rather than money or utility, are the right primitive for such economies, grounded on the verified results of section 3.

4.1 Agents Without Stable Interests

In artificial intelligence the standard model of an agent is precisely the framework's: an entity that perceives and acts, and a rational agent is one that acts to maximize its expected utility (Russell and Norvig 2021). Agent-based computational economics studies economies as populations of such interacting agents, deriving aggregate outcomes from their local interactions rather than from an equilibrium assumed in advance (Tesfatsion and Judd 2006). But the framework's definition of an agent as a consistent chooser is exactly the condition that language-model agents may fail. Recent work measuring the logical consistency of large language models finds that their judgments are frequently intransitive, order-sensitive, and otherwise incoherent, so that they do not reliably behave as if maximizing any single stable utility (Liu et al. 2024). An entity whose choices do not consistently reveal one interest is, on the revealed-preference reading of section 3.1, an entity of which that theory can say little.

This is where the framework claims an advantage. Where standard theory needs a stable utility to get started, interest-based economics treats the absence or instability of a clear interest as a first-class case: interests can be unknown, plural, or in flux, and coverage can still be assessed against whatever interests an agent does have. Artificial agents also make vivid the framework's claim that buying is only one satisfier among many. An agent whose interests are informational or computational may have them met directly, without acquiring money to acquire a good; and a population of agents that can represent and exchange their interests richly and cheaply is one that has moved toward Hayek's information-rich limit (section 3.2), where money's function as a compressor of dispersed knowledge has less to do. The framework's suggestion is not that money disappears but that its share of the coordination work falls as the informational premise that made it valuable weakens.

4.2 Abundance and the Return to Direct Provision

The second forward claim concerns humans rather than machines. When aggregate wealth is very high, the framework holds, the case for satisfying basic human interests through cash routed via markets weakens relative to provisioning those interests directly through public goods, because what the theory counts is coverage of interests, not monetary throughput. Two of section 3's verified results support the move. Samuelson's public-goods theorem shows that a class of interests is met by non-rival goods that no market price mechanism will supply optimally, so that above some level of wealth the efficient way to raise coverage of those interests is direct provision rather than transfer (Samuelson 1954). And the cash-versus-in-kind literature catalogues the conditions, imperfect information, paternalistic concern for particular interests, and political-economy constraints, under which providing a good in kind dominates handing over its cash equivalent (Currie and Gahvari 2008). Rawls's primary goods supply a normative vocabulary for the bundle a just society secures directly rather than leaving to the market (Rawls 1971).

The framework reads extreme wealth, of the kind an agent-heavy productive economy might generate, as shifting the optimal mix toward the direct coverage of basic interests. The argument is not that markets are dispensable but that their comparative advantage, coordinating dispersed interests under scarce information and scarce goods, is exactly what abundance and information-richness erode, and that at the limit the moral content of interestism, satisfy agents' interests, is better discharged by provisioning needs than by maximizing the money with which needs might be bought.

5. Objections and Replies

5.1 The Circularity Objection

If an interest is whatever consistent choice reveals, then the theory cannot criticize a choice as failing to serve the agent's interest, and interestism collapses into “satisfy whatever agents choose.” The framework's reply is that preferences are defeasible indicators of interests, not definitions of them, and it imports the adaptive-preference and constructed-preference results (Nussbaum 2000; Lichtenstein and Slovic 2006) to drive a wedge between revealed preference and real interest. This reply is essential to the theory's critical edge and to its handling of manipulable and unstable choosers, but it purchases that edge at a price paid in section 6: once revealed preference no longer fixes the interest, something else must, and the theory owes an account of what.

5.2 The Paternalism Objection

A theory that distinguishes an agent's real interests from its expressed choices, and that recommends provisioning those interests directly, appears to license overriding agents in the name of interests they have not avowed. The framework concedes the danger and treats it as the standing risk of any needs-based or capability-based view. It owes, and does not yet fully have, a principled threshold separating cases where expressed choice is authoritative from cases where a manipulated or adaptive preference may be discounted.

5.3 The Hayekian Objection

The whole argument of section 4 turns on the premise that an ecosystem can become information-rich enough to erode money's coordinating value, and Hayek's own position is that the dispersion of knowledge is a permanent feature of social life, so that the premise is never satisfied and money remains essential. The framework accepts that this is the load-bearing empirical bet of the theory. Its answer is not to deny Hayek's point for human economies but to identify agent-based settings as the first case in which the premise is even arguably approached, and to rest the strength of the forward claims on how far that approach actually goes, which is an open question rather than a settled result.

5.4 The Demarcation of Agency

The definition of an agent as any consistent chooser overgenerates. A thermostat chooses consistently; so does a simple script. If interestism owes the satisfaction of interests to every consistent chooser, its moral demand is absurd. The framework's available responses, that morally weighty agency requires a hierarchy of interests (section 3.4), a capacity for the interest to go unmet in ways that matter, or a richness the thermostat lacks, each reintroduce a threshold the austere definition was meant to avoid. This is treated as a genuinely open problem rather than a solved one.

5.5 The Is–Ought Objection

From the descriptive claim that economies are driven by interests, nothing normative follows, so interestism's move to the obligation to satisfy interests looks like a non-sequitur. The framework does not claim the derivation. Interestism is posited as an independent normative commitment, and the descriptive theory of interests is offered as the account of what that commitment, once accepted, directs us to attend to. The pairing is deliberate and its two halves are separable, much as a theory of welfare and a duty to promote it are separable.

6. Open Problems

Four problems structure the research program the framework projects. First, and most pressing for section 4, is the empirical question whether artificial agents have stable interests at all: the evidence that language-model agents choose inconsistently (Liu et al. 2024) is suggestive but early, and whether such agents have interests that are merely unstable, genuinely plural, or absent is unsettled and pivotal, since the framework's advertised advantage over utility-based theory depends on the answer. Second is the individuation and measurement problem: having separated real interest from revealed preference in section 5.1, the theory must say how interests and their coverage are to be identified and measured without either collapsing back into revealed preference or being simply stipulated. Third is the status of the abundance claim: that extreme wealth shifts the optimum toward direct provision is supported by the public-goods and cash-versus-in-kind results but is not entailed by them, and its scope is a question of mechanism design and empirical political economy rather than of the theorems alone. Fourth, and underlying the others, much of section 4 rests on a thin and recent literature and should be read as a program to be carried out rather than a body of established results.
